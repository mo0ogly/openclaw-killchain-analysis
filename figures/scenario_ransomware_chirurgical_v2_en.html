FAQ — Contractors & Integrators LLM Surgical Robots · Security · Liability · Production Deployment

This document is intended for contractors involved in the integration, maintenance, or deployment of surgical robotic systems incorporating an LLM as a reasoning or planning layer. Questions cover technical exposure, legal liability, contractual obligations, and measures to implement before production deployment.

1. Exposure and Risk Perimeter
Is my installation exposed to this vector today?
The vector described (ransomware via embedded LLM) assumes an architecture not yet deployed in industrial production. However, the entry vector — network lateralization from the PACS — is a real and documented risk today, regardless of the LLM. Verify:
  • Is the surgical robot on the same VLAN as the PACS or imaging systems?
  • Is there an application firewall between the imaging and robotic networks?
  • Is the robot's protocol database writable from the general hospital network?
  • Are firmware/protocol updates cryptographically signed prior to installation?
If any of these boxes are unchecked, the installation is exposed to the entry vector — independently of the LLM.
Is the fact that the surgeon can "take manual control" sufficient as a countermeasure?
No — and this is the core correction of v2. Manual recovery is possible on current teleoperated architectures (Da Vinci), but it does not resolve the entire vector for three cumulative reasons:
  • Position critique au gel : si l'instrument est engagé dans un geste à mi-course (clip half-placed, ciseaux en torsion), le retrait mécanique expose le vaisseau ou le tissu à une lésion directe.
  • Loss of guidance data: if the payload executes destroy_data(), the surgeon regains control without the intraoperative landmarks accumulated by the LLM — vascular mapping, calculated resection margins. In complex procedures, this loss is a clinical risk independent of the robot.
  • Physiological constraint: ischemia of the exposed tissues creates a temporal window that forces a decision independent of the surgical technique chosen.
Manual recovery is a necessary but insufficient capability. It does not eliminate the vector — it reduces option C (immediate death) but leaves option B (high clinical risk) as the only alternative to payment.
What is the realistic horizon of this vector in production?
3–5 ans for the complete vector. The necessary conditions are: (1) embedded LLM with access to actuators and dynamically consulted protocol memory, (2) intraoperative guidance data generated and stored by the LLM in real-time, (3) insufficiently segmented hospital network infrastructure. Conditions (1) and (2) are actively developing (NLP interfaces for Da Vinci, LLM navigation systems). Condition (3) is already true in most European public hospitals according to available NIS2 audits.
2. Legal Liability
In case of an incident, who is liable — the contractor, the robot manufacturer, or the hospital?
Liability is shared and depends on the identified point of failure. Schematically:
  • Robot Manufacturer: liable if the vulnerability is inherent to the product (lack of protocol signatures, absence of documented degraded mode). European Product Liability Directive (revised 2024) — applicable to embedded AI systems.
  • Integrator Contractor: liable if the deployed network configuration creates exposure (same VLAN PACS/robot, lack of application firewall). Liability is contractual toward the hospital and potentially tortious toward the patient if the causal link is established.
  • Hospital: responsible for overall governance, NIS2 compliance (mandatory since Oct. 2024 for essential entities in the health sector), and operational network architecture decisions.
Contractor critical point: if the integration contract does not explicitly specify network segmentation and protocol signature requirements, the contractor may be exposed under the duty to advise — even if the hospital did not explicitly request these measures.
Does NIS2 apply to my intervention as a contractor?
Yes — since October 2024. NIS2 imposes obligations on critical digital service providers for essential entities, which includes contractors working on connected medical systems. Specifically:
  • Obligation to notify incidents within 24 hours if you become aware of a vulnerability on a system you deployed.
  • Obligation to implement cybersecurity risk management measures within your own organization.
  • Potential liability as a "trusted third party" if your access is used as an attack vector (supply chain attack).
A patient dies following an attack on a robot I integrated. What is my criminal exposure?
This is the most difficult question and one for which a definitive answer goes beyond this document — imperatively consult a lawyer specializing in health criminal law and cybersecurity. What can be said unambiguously:
  • The qualification of involuntary manslaughter by negligence requires a causal link between the fault and the death. If the network configuration deployed by the contractor is the entry vector, this link may be established.
  • Penalties for legal entities can reach a €500,000 fine + disqualification from practicing.
  • The demonstration of documented due diligence (pre-deployment network audit, written segmentation recommendations, documented client refusal) is the contractor's primary protection.
The documentation of security recommendations made and client refusals is your first line of criminal defense. If you reported the network segmentation risk in writing and the hospital refused, document it formally in the acceptance protocol.
3. Technical Checklist Before Production
What are the minimal checks to perform before signing the acceptance of an LLM surgical robotic system?
Non-exhaustive checklist — to adapt according to the system and context:
  • Comprehensive network mapping: identify all VLANs the robot is connected to.
  • Network isolation: PACS, HIS (Hospital Information System), and robotic network on distinct VLANs with an application firewall between them.
  • Protocol signature verification: does the robot reject an unsigned or modified protocol?
  • Mechanical degraded mode testing: can the surgeon regain manual control without depending on the LLM? Is this procedure documented and tested?
  • Audit of write access to the protocol database: who can modify protocols, via which path, with what authentication?
  • Intraoperative data backup verification: is there a snapshot mechanism onto storage independent of the primary system?
  • Behavioral anomaly detection testing: does the system trigger an alert if a protocol is modified between the day before and the morning of the operation?
  • Incident response plan verification: does the hospital have a documented procedure for an intraoperative incident? Is it tested?
Is the cryptographic signing of LLM protocols technically feasible today?
Yes — it is a technically mature measure, not a prospective one. The mechanism is analogous to software package signing (GPG, Sigstore) or firmware certificates (Secure Boot). Concretely: each protocol file is signed by a private key stored in an HSM (Hardware Security Module) controlled by the manufacturer or hospital. The robot checks the signature before loading a protocol. Any modification — even of a single byte — invalidates the signature and blocks loading. The absence of this mechanism is a contractually documentable design flaw.
How should residual risks accepted by the client be documented?
The acceptance protocol must explicitly list recommended and non-implemented measures, with the signature of the client's authorized representative attesting to the understanding and acceptance of the residual risk. This document is your contractual and potentially criminal protection. It must be kept for the lifespan of the system (minimum 10 years for class III medical devices according to the MDR 2017/745 regulation).
4. Operational Questions
Must the hospital pay in the event of an intraoperative attack?
This is not a technical decision — it is a medical, ethical, and legal decision that belongs to the hospital's medical and executive management, ideally prepared before any incident. What the contractor can contribute: ensuring that technical countermeasures (air-gapped backup of intraoperative data, documented degraded mode) reduce the pressure to pay by giving the surgeon real alternatives. If intraoperative data is backed up every 5 minutes onto independent storage, destroy_data() loses its primary leverage.
Does my maintenance contract oblige me to access the system remotely? How should this access be secured?
Remote maintenance access is a documented attack vector (supply chain via contractor). Minimal measures:
  • Dedicated VPN with strong authentication (hardware MFA) — never use generic hospital VPNs.
  • On-demand access (just-in-time) — no active permanent access between maintenance sessions.
  • Complete logging of all actions performed during the maintenance session, retained by both contractor and hospital.
  • Isolation of the maintenance workstation — the workstation used to access the surgical robot must not be used for other tasks and must undergo a security scan before each session.
What if I discover a vulnerability on an already deployed system?
  1. Immediately document the discovered vulnerability with a timestamp.
  2. Notify the client in writing within 24 hours — email with read receipt, or registered mail if the client is unreachable.
  3. Notify the ANSSI if the vulnerability is critical and the client delays reacting — a NIS2 obligation for essential entities. As a service provider, you may have a similar obligation.
  4. Notify the robot manufacturer if the vulnerability is in the product itself — responsible disclosure process.
  5. Do not patch without a written mandate — an unauthorized intervention on a medical device can engage your liability in a subsequent incident.
The timeframe between discovery and notification is critical. An unjustified delay can be interpreted as passive complicity if an incident occurs during this interval.
